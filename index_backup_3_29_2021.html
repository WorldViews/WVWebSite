Welcome to WorldViews

WorldViews was founded in 2015 with the vision of connecting people by helping them share views, stories and adventures from around the world. Many WorldViews projects and activities have involved 360 video, virtual earths, or remote telepresence but our core mission is not fundamentally technological - it is to foster mutual understanding and an appreciation that all of humanity is one family sharing one Earth. Our core values are diversity, inclusivity, cooperation, peacemaking and the spreading of love and inspiration. Organizationally we are informal, with the feel of an educational club, oriented around play, where members learn through involvement in WorldViews projects.

TeleViewer

This activity is to build cool server and client software for furthering the WorldViews mission. All our software is open source, and built on open source. Currently the main efforts are building a client virtual earth browser called TeleViewer, which can show many layers of information, videos, and live video feeds. TeleViewer is built upon the open source CesiumJS. Another part of the current development is to develop a server to support many WorldViews capabilities. We currently support serving the TeleViewer using either nodejs, django, python flask, or a simple python server. We primarily are focusing on flask, and use the mongodb.

Kinetech Arts & DanceHack

WorldViews has been involved with the Arts & Technology nonprofit Kinetech Arts since 2015, first through participation in DanceHack, and by Don Kimber serving on their board of directors since 2017.  DanceHack teams digital artists, technologists, musicians and dancers on performance projects.  Our first project was with whirling dancer Raquel Santiago.  Don created a 3D animation, expressing Raquel’s spiritual interpretation of her dancing together with a fabric simulation of her skirt, using three.js.  A bluetooth IMU sensor synchronized the animation with Raquel’s orientation as she danced.  Another year, we captured 360 video of dancers performing around a central cone.  Live or recorded video projected onto the cone provided a panoramic display.  This led to the patent of a Panoramic Portal device that could connect people at different locations through 360 video.  A 2018 design study for a possible DanceHack project led to Reactive Video, in which a movement artist could perform with a video player that stayed synchronized with their movement.  Don Kimber and Kinetech Art's Weidong Yang co-chaired the 2017 ACM Multimedia Interactive Art exhibition, and hosted an Arts & Tech event for the Bay Area Multimedia Forum.


Social Modeling and Simulations

In collaboration with USFCA, Kineviz and Kinetech Arts, we're currently working on a system for agent based social modeling and simulation.  We are building a JavaScript simulation engine with interfaces through ObservableHQ and the Kineviz GraphXR visualization platform.  A number of different models are under development, including for cognitive and emotional contagion (e.g. effect on happiness from random acts of kindness) and for spread of ideologies and polarization in social networks.  We plan to explore models for both synthetic and real data sets.  A short term goal is to describe and compare some different mechanisms that can account for ideological polarization - “the divide” of our current society, and how different social media affect this.  The models will be available and used for interactive simulations, as well as by artists in the Kinetech Arts community for artistic performance or installation.

Reactive Video

Standard video players have no user model, and simply play, pause, scrub, etc. in reaction to UI controls.  Reactive video, like a good instructor, tracks the user, and plays the video in a manner appropriate to the user’s activity.  For a physical training activity, say Tai Chi, the player synchronizes the video to match the user’s movement.  One practical value of this is that the user doesn’t fuss so much with a UI to get the video to pause or repeat.  An experiential value is that it changes video from a passive experience of watching and following, to one of being connected with the video, for a more immersive and engaging experience.  The system uses a Kinect or OpenPose to track body movement for the reference training video and Kinect or Posenet for the live user. There are several use modes.  In one, a user simply watches the video of a movement, and attempts to passively follow the movement.  Subsets of tracked joints can be chosen and used to find a best alignment of user and reference movements (with adjustment made for different body sizes.)  Visual indicators are drawn on the video to give feedback about alignment and to show the paths joints should follow, and changes made to a musical or soundscape background for feedback and motivation.  Afterwards, graphs of the alignment as a function of time through a movement can be examined by the user or an instructor.

 

UIST 2020, “Reactive Video: Adaptive Video Playback Based on User Motion for Supporting Physical Activity”, C. Clarke, D. Cavdir, P. Chiu, L. Denoue, D. Kimber.  paper video: 15min 5min

 

 


RhythmSticks

In collaboration with Derrick Kikuchi at Reach and Teach, and occupational therapist Sydney Shiroyama, who uses taiko drumming therapeutically for special needs clients and communities, I’m developing software and hardware based rhythm games.  The core of the system is a MIDI based rhythm engine that can synchronize with users in playing rhythms.  As with reactive video, there is a passive mode in which users follow a rhythm pattern and get feedback on how well they are doing.  In another mode the system seeks to follow the flow of the user.  The engine runs in a web page, shows the rhythm patterns radially as on a clock, and can play preset songs, or songs can be entered using drum languages of various drum communities, such as kuchi shoga for Taiko.  The web page can be connected with midi devices, such as keyboards, drum pads, or devices designed for air drumming, such as senstroke. It can also connect to and control external arduino devices, such as a “RhythmStick”, that has lights showing beats, and using an accelerometer to detect drum strokes.  The system is on display at Reach and Teach, where Derrick is observing how users interact with it using drums in the store, such as the “sun moon star” drum pictured above. 

 


MUSE

MUSE is a high level vision with several key themes.  One is to ‘move the web browser out of the display and into the room’.  A MUSE venue is a virtual or real room (e.g. black box theatre) that is a sort of ‘program browser’ that can display exhibits, programs, games, support sharing of media content, for programs, games, simulations, etc.  Together with high school science teacher Derrick McMillen, we created a venue called Imaginarium, which is an imaginary deck of a starship, with displays, controls, a central stage, and windows.  The ceiling can be used as a planetarium to show immersive videos.  We implemented a virtual imaginarium using threeJS and WebVR.   We also set up a lab with imersive displays to be a physical imaginarium.

Our first detailed program was a presentation of the ClimateMusic Project performace of the composition Climate, by Erik Ian Walker.  This emotionally powerful piece conveys climate changes from the year 1800 to present, with alternative projections up to the year 2200, based on different scenarios for reduction of CO2 emissions.  The program included visualizations of climate data - CO2 levels, temperature, and energy balance, and users could interactively choose among visualizations. A basic animation was 3D animated graphs.  Another was a virtual earth with color and opacity of atmosphere indicating temperate and CO2 level, and particle streams indicationg energy balance.  Another visualization was a dancer, producing streaming particle trails with color and density indicating data.  In VR the participant could also dance and see particles streaming from their dancing.  The VR program was demonstrated at a ClimateMusic event at the San Francisco opera center.  In physical venue, at an FXPAL lab room, participants could dance and be tracked by a Kinect, and see the particles streaming from their avatar on the main display.

The MUSE architecture emphasizes separation of media content of a program from the displays, speakers, stages, etc. of a venue in which the program is performed.  Then during a performance, the channels are mapped to rendering devices.  The mapping can be chosen automatically (e.g. with default video getting mapped onto the most prominent display), or these mappings can be explicitly set for a performance, or dynamically changed. This is similar in spirit to logically separating the data content of a web page from the fields and precise rendering styles in which it is displayed.


Polly & Remote Telepresence

During years of working on 360 video, Don Kimber had always wanted to do a project where someone stuck in a hospital could connect to and control a robot with a 360 camera or a steerable camera.  When he started working on such a project it occurred to him that he’d rather connect them with a human guide.  The remote person could be like a parrot riding the guide’s shoulder.  The Polly project implemented various incarnations of this idea, with the most advanced using a phone on a gimbal that could provide stabilization and view control.  Guides carrying Polly devices and viewers could meet in a google hangout, and the remote participants could use an interface in the hangout to control the camera.  A high point of this project was the chance to work with Henry Evans and provide him with virtual tours.

ACVR 2014, “Polly: Telepresence from a Guide’s Shoulder”, D. Kimber, P. Proppe, S. Kratz, J. Vaughan, B. Liew, D. Severns, W. Su.  pdf  Henry Evans video.


360 Video Virtual Tours

I’ve worked for many years on 360 video tours, where panoramic video is collected along paths and reindexed by position.  This allows users to take virtual tours by moving along paths and looking in any direction.  It’s similar to street view, or many types of 360 tours (e.g. QTVR) except the user can move forward or backward along a path, and see video of the movement, rather than transitions from one fixed point to another.  Some versions of our viewer supported an HMD, and joystick control, and when users reached path intersections, they could seamlessly choose a path with the joystick.  Mostly though, I’ve focused on web delivered virtual tours, viewable on screen, phone or tablet.  The imaging technology has matured, and now high-quality video is easily captured with consumer cameras.  The spatial imagining has also improved.  Initially we used a combination of hand registration for indoor, and GPS for outdoor.  Now GPS is ubiquitous, and phones can perform SLAM.  Recently we used OpenVSLAM, which produces highly accurate spatial indices.  Follow on projects in this area have been part of WorldViews and presented with its viewers.

ACM MM 2001 “FlyAbout:Spatially Indexed Panoramic Video” D. Kimber, J. Foote, S. Lertsithichai. pdf video


Mirror Worlds

A mirror world is a virtual model of a real physical space, which may be updated with various sensors or data feeds to indicate things about the state of the world.  It is like a living 3D map and has many uses.  A mirror world of a conference venue may be used to market the venue, to plan for a conference, by presenters or conference attendees to plan presentations or itineraries.  During the conference, it can be used as a portal for remote people to virtually attend the conference or can be used afterwards to show a recording of the conference.  I’ve been involved in creating Mirror Worlds for several venues and locations, and platforms with several different 3D viewers - Google Earth, Unity3D, OpenSceneGraph and VRML.

ACM MM2012  “Through the Looking-Glass: Mirror Worlds for Augmented Awareness & Capability”, D. Kimber, J. Shingu, J. Vaughan.   pdf video

ACM MM2007, “DOTS: Support for Effective Video Surveillance”, A. Girgensohn, D. Kimber, J. Vaughan, T. Yang, F. Shipman, T. Turner, E. Rieffel, L. Wilcox, F. Chen and T. Dunnigan. pdf video

ClimateMusic Project

We seek to make climate science personal

Combining the talents and expertise of world class scientists, composers, musicians, artists, and technology visionaries, we enable the creation and staging of science-guided music and visual experiences to inspire people to engage actively on the issue of climate change.

Music is familiar, accessible, and—for most people—much easier to relate to than articles or lectures about the climate crisis. We created The ClimateMusic Project to harness this universal language to tell the urgent story of climate change to broad and diverse audiences in a way that resonates, educates, and motivates.
